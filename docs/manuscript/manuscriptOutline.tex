\section*{Introduction}
% <><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><>
\begin{enumerate}

% Paragraph 1
\item The most frequently observed biological impact of climate change is major changes in phenology \citep{parmesan_globally_2003,cleland_shifting_2007,lieth_phenology_1974,woolway_phenological_2021,menzel_european_2006}
  \begin{enumerate}
  
  \item Shifts in spring and autumn phenology modifies when seasons start and end \citep{duputie_phenological_2015}.
  
  \item Understanding the consequences of these shifts requires understanding how much and why growing seasons have changed \citep{duputie_phenological_2015}.
  
  \item Spring phenology advances, \citep{wolfe_climate_2005,chmielewski_response_2001,fu_recent_2014} driven by temperature \citep{chuine_why_2010,cleland_shifting_2007,penuelas_responses_2001}
  
  \item Autumn phenology delays, but much less than spring \citep{gallinat_autumn_2015,jeong_macroscale_2014} and driven by temperature and photoperiod \citep{cooke_dynamic_2012,flynn_temperature_2018,korner_phenology_2010,delpierre_temperate_2016}
  
  
  \end{enumerate}
  
% Paragraph 2
\item Shifts in spring and autumn phenology supports the assumption that longer growing seasons increase tree growth \citep{keenan_net_2014, stridbeck_partly_2022}
  \begin{enumerate}
  
  \item Recent research shows that this may not be the case \citep{dow_warm_2022,green_limits_2022,silvestro_longer_2023,wang_seasonal_2025,matula_shifts_2023,wang_seasonal_2025}
  
  \item \cite{dow_warm_2022} showed that trees did not grow more or faster despite warmer and earlier springs at the regional scale
  
  \item \cite{cabon_cross-biome_2022} showed a decoupling between GPP and carbon storage at the global scale.
  
  \item If trees don't grow as we expect, it could affect forest carbon cycle projections \citep{richardson_climate_2013,swidrak_comparing_2013} and subsequent climate solutions \citep{green_limits_2022}
  
  \end{enumerate}
  
% Paragraph 
\item Understanding these finds requires answering why trees do not grow more despite longer growing seasons.
  \begin{enumerate}
  
  \item We have a poor understanding of carbon allocation to above-ground biomass, despite it being one of the largest carbon sink \citep{cabon_cross-biome_2022}.
  
  \item Linear representation of growth as a function of carbon assimilation is wrong \citep{cabon_cross-biome_2022}
  
  \item Trees may be source or sink limited, which emphasizes the importance of understanding the temperature sensitivity relationship between growth and photosynthesis \citep{gessler_beyond_2024}.
  
  \item Growth may be more sensitive to varying environmental conditions than photosynthesis, which could mislead models \citep{cabon_cross-biome_2022,cabon_water_2020,muller_water_2011,peters_turgor_2021}
  
  \item While warmer spring temperatures advance the onset of cell production, that alone does not increase wood production with water playing a more decisive role on wood prod \citep{wang_seasonal_2025}.
  
  \item Complex nature of climate change makes understanding the internal physiological and external constraints to growth critical \citep{almagro_longterm_2025,cabon_cross-biome_2022,wolkovich_why_2025}
  \end{enumerate}

% Paragraph 
\item How trees internal programming and their susceptibility to environmental conditions could constrain their growth?
  \begin{enumerate}
  \item Growth as any other biological mechanism is genetically controlled, and will thus vary by species \citep{howe_genotype_2003,wolkovich_why_2025,silvestro_roots_2025}
  
  \item Tree plasticity is a highly variable trait that changes with species' life history strategies \citep{cuny_couplings_2019,michelot_comparing_2012}, affecting how flexible their growth is in response to varying environmental conditions.
  
  \item While $\text{CO}_2$ concentrations increased, drought, heat waves, competition, insect outbreaks and spring frosts---factors projected to change in frequency and severity with a warming climate---can offset the benefits of longer seasons \citep{tyree_xylem_2002, choat_triggers_2018, li_widespread_2023,trenberth_global_2014,intergovernmental_panel_on_climate_change_detection_2014,chiang_evidence_2021,polgar_leafout_2011,reinmann_compensatory_2023}.
  
  \item Droughts can act as a severe growth constraint \citep{kang_earlier_2023} by stopping growth early \citep{dox_severe_2022} or inducing tissue mortality \citep{li_widespread_2023}.
  
  \item Intra- and inter-specific competition for nutrients, water and light can also offset a tree's capacity to take advantage of extra days in the season \citep{cleland_effects_2024}.

  \end{enumerate}
  
% Paragraph 
\item Understanding the constraints to growth requires defining the growing season and growth.
  \begin{enumerate}
  
  \item Different definitions of GS: we will use calendar (number of days) and thermal season (window of opportunity of thermally favorable meteorological conditions for plant growth) \citep{korner_four_2023} (Figure \ref{fig:GSfig})
  
    \item Calendar season only accounts for the number of days in the season, implying a stable growth rate throughout the season. 
  
  \item This may reveal a role of some neglected cooler and shorter days in the season that have a weak influence on the thermal season (Figure \ref{fig:GSfig}a).
  
  \item Growth rate changes with temperature, which the thermal season accounts for and should therefore better predict growth responses.
  
  \item Wood formation (xylogenesis) is the allocation of carbon for long-term storage \citep{etzold_number_2022,silvestro_roots_2025}, and the radial growth increments are represented through tree rings \citep{plomion_wood_2001}
  
  \item Allometries using tree DBH and height have a coarse temporal scale and can miss extreme events affecting growth  \citep[e.g.,][]{meyer_mathematical_1940,saunders_height-diameter_2008}.
  
  \item Tree rings are more precise growth proxies, which we use here \citep{gazol_forest_2018,manzanedo_towards_2019}
  
  \end{enumerate}
  
% Paragraph 
\item Growth drivers differences across species need to be considered.
  \begin{enumerate} 
  
  \item Phenology and the relationship between growth and growing season length varies heavily across species \citep{etzold_number_2022}
  
  \item Pooling all species together in carbon sequestrations models is a weakness \citep{green_limits_2022,cabon_cross-biome_2022,wolkovich_why_2025}
  
  \item We propose to use ground-based leaf phenology observations across several species to address this issue \citep{piao_plant_2019}
  
  \item These observations provide valuable insights of the growth temporality of trees not suitable for experimental trials \citep{wolkovich_why_2025}
  
  \item Cambial phenology in diffuse-porous trees is closely synchronized with leaf phenology \citep{marchand_timing_2021,silvestro_roots_2025,suzuki_phenological_1996}, so we can use them to as a reliable proxy for start and end of the growing season \citep{stridbeck_partly_2022} 
  
  \end{enumerate}
  
\item Local adaptation could inform growth responses of trees under future climate change \citep{soolanayakanahally_timing_2013,wolkovich_why_2025}.
  \begin{enumerate} 
  \item Provenance refers to the geographic and climatic origin of a population \citep{aitken_time_2016,housset_tree_2018}
  
  \item Trees from northern provenances sometimes have later start of season \citep{zeng_weak_2024} and often earlier end of season phenology $=$ shorter growing season \citep{way_photoperiod_2015,vitasse_interaction_2014}.
  
  \item This is especially true for budset---marks the end of primary growth---, with its phenotypic variance being strongly related to provenance and photoperiod acting as the primary cue \citep{aitken_time_2016,soolanayakanahally_timing_2013,way_photoperiod_2015}.
  
  \item Thus, common gardens with provenances across a latitudinal gradient can inform tree growth responses to future climatic conditions.
  
  \end{enumerate} 

% Paragraph 6
\item To answer if trees grow more with longer growing seasons, we related three years of leaf phenology and growth data across four species and four provenances in a common garden on juvenile trees. Our objectives are:
  \begin{enumerate}
  \item Determine if trees grow more with longer thermal vs calendar season (ref: conceptual figure).
  \item Understand the calendar growing season relationship with growth and how it relates to the SOS and EOS.
  \item Determine how growth changes across years, provenances and individual trees.
  \end{enumerate}
\end{enumerate}

\section*{Results}
\begin{enumerate}
\item Does tree growth increase with longer growing seasons?
\item Did an earlier start and later end of season increase growth? 
\item Does tree growth increase with longer growing seasons and is it consistent across species and provenances?
\item How did growth change across years, and how did it relate to climate?
\end{enumerate}

\begin{enumerate}
\item Summary basic information
  \begin{enumerate}
  
  \item Wildchrokie includes four deciduous tree species from four sites with leaf phenology data spaning three years (2018 to 2020) for a total of 239 leafout and 
  230 budset observations, yielding 
  227 complete growing seasons (GS) for 
  81 individuals. 
    
  \item Mean summer temperature were similar across years with 2019 being the coolest (22.38$^\circ$C), 2020 the hottest (23.07$^\circ$C), and 2018 in between (22.85$^\circ$C)
    
  \item The calendar and thermal seasons ranged from 
  90 days and 
  1465.1 GDD ({\Ball} in 2018) to 
  173 days and 2513.6 GDD ({\Ainc} in 2018)

  \item The earliest leafout day of year was 
  22-Apr ({\Ainc} in
  2019) and the latest,
  05-Jun (also {\Ainc}, but in
  2020). 
  The earliest budset day of year was 
  13-Aug ({\Ball} in
  2018) and the latest, 
  24-Oct ({\Ainc} in
  2018) 
  
  \item No drought for the three years, except for in 2020, where a moderate summer drought ramped up to a severe drought in September.
  
  \item Annual ring widths across years varied from 
  0.39 mm ({\Ball} in 2020) to 
  13.55 mm ({\Bpop} in 2018) (Figure \ref{fig:wcboxplot}). 
 
\end{enumerate}

% Question 1 --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- ---
\item Does tree growth increase with longer growing seasons?
  \begin{enumerate}

  \item Longer growing seasons increased growth for all species, but varied depending on the metric used to measure it.

  \item Longer thermal season increased growth for {\Bpap} 
  ($\beta = $ 
  0.44, $UI_{90}$
  [0.18, 
  0.7]),
  {\Bpop} 
  ($\beta = $ 
  0.22, $UI_{90}$
  [0.07, 
  0.36]), and {\Ball} 
  ($\beta = $ 
  0.13, $UI_{90}$
  [0.01, 
  0.24]), but {\Ainc} had a weak effect, but trended in the same direction 
  ($\beta = $ 
  0.07, $UI_{90}$
  [-0.04, 
  0.17]) (Figure \ref{fig:wcmuplotbspp}a and e) (Table\ref{tab:wc_slope_table}).
 
  \item {\Ainc} grew more with longer calendar seasons ($\beta = $ 
  0.05, $UI_{90}$
  [0, 
  0.1]), but of the three species that responded to thermal season, only {\Bpap} also grew more with longer calendar seasons ($\beta = $ 
  0.18, $UI_{90}$
  [0.05, 
  0.32]).
  \item {\Ball} ($\beta = $ 
  0.03, $UI_{90}$
  [-0.02, 
  0.09]), and {\Bpop} ($\beta = $ 
  0.05, $UI_{90}$
  [-0.02, 
  0.13]) trended in the same direction (Figure \ref{fig:wcmuplotbspp}b and f) (Table \ref{tab:wc_slope_table}).
  
  \item Results with basal area increment (BAI) for the GDD model were broadly similar to ring width (see Table \ref{tab:BAItab}) 
  
  \item On a scaled axis, both predictors were similarly predictive (Figure\ref{fig:bsppZscored}). 
  \end{enumerate}
  
% Question 2 --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- ---
\item Did a later start and end of season increase growth?
  \begin{enumerate}
  \item A longer calendar season increased growth for two species, but only one grew more with an earlier start of season ({\Ainc}: $\beta = $ 
  -0.07, $UI_{90}$
  [-0.14, 
  -0.01]) (Figure \ref{fig:wcmuplotbspp}c and g) (Table \ref{tab:wc_slope_table}).
  
  \item {\Ball} which did not grow more with longer calendar season, grew less with an earlier start of season, unlike our prediction ($\beta = $ 
  0.1, $UI_{90}$
  [0, 
  0.2]) (Figure \ref{fig:wcmuplotbspp}c and g) (Table \ref{tab:wc_slope_table}).
  
  \item Three species grew more with a later end of season ({\Ball}: $\beta = $ 
  0.12, $UI_{90}$
  [0.05, 
  0.18], 
  {\Bpop}: $\beta = $ 
  0.1, $UI_{90}$
  [0.02, 
  0.17]), with {\Bpap} 
  ($\beta = $ 
  0.21, $UI_{90}$
  [0.09, 
  0.33])
  being the only one of those three that also grew more with longer calendar season (Figure \ref{fig:wcmuplotbspp}d and h) (Table \ref{tab:wc_slope_table}).
  
  \item The estimates were unchanged when using the full (SOS $n =$ 239 and $n =$ 230) or restricted datasets ($n=$227) (Figure \ref{fig:wcSOSEOScomparison}a). 
  
  \end{enumerate}

% Question 3 --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- ---
\item How did tree growth change across years, provenances and tree ids?
  \begin{enumerate}
  
  \item We did not find any clear effect of year on growth (Figure \ref{fig:wcmuplotayear}) and there is no clear differences of growth looking across species (Figure \ref{fig:wcxyGDD} and Figure \ref{fig:wcxyGSL})
  
  \item Growth did not change across any provenances (Figure \ref{fig:wcmuplotasite}) (Table \ref{tab:wc_asite_table}), and the variation across sites ($\sigma = $
  0.18, $UI_{90}$
  [0.02, 
  0.5) and tree ids ($\sigma = $ 
  0.17, $UI_{90}$
  [0.05, 
  0.27]) was similar

  \end{enumerate}


\end{enumerate}

\section*{Discussion}
\begin{enumerate}
\item What we did, how we did it and what we found
  \begin{enumerate}
  
  \item We used 81 juvenile trees of four species across four provenances to understand if longer growing seasons using leaf phenology increased growth using tree rings
  
  \item We found that longer growing seasons increased growth for all species, but varied depending on the metric 
  
  \item Thermal season was a better predictor for growth than the calendar season
  \end{enumerate}

\item Why thermal season better predicts growth than the calendar season?
  \begin{enumerate}
  
  \item Thermal weights growth days better than calendar season---trees don't grow at the same speed all year round
  
  \item Cool and short spring days count for less in the thermal season compared to the calendar season
  
  \item {\Ainc} responding to GSL$>$GDD may be able to take advantage of cool and short days in spring more than other species
  
  \end{enumerate}
  
\item SOS and EOS to disentangle the calendar season effect on growth
  \begin{enumerate}
  
  \item {\Ainc} grow more with GSL and earlier SOS, meaning that most growth happens in spring.
  
  \item On the contrary, {\Bpap} driven by EOS rather than SOS
  
  \item {\Ball} Decrease in growth with earlier SOS and increase with later EOS counterinteract and lead to no response to GSL
  \end{enumerate}

\item Our trees grew more with longer seasons in absence of external constraints
  \begin{enumerate}

  \item When seasons are longer, but not drier, trees can fully take advantage of these extra days
  
  \item Our study design controlled light competition---alleviating competition for light as a growth constraint
  
  \end{enumerate}


\item No effect of provenance
  \begin{enumerate}
  \item Our results suggest that local adaption on growth is weak under current conditions
  
  \item Though our latitudinal gradient is narrow
  \end{enumerate}

\item Limitations
  \begin{enumerate}
  \item Low sample size and not a fully crossed study design
  
  \item Didn't account for extreme temperatures (though no extended heat wave periods in our study)
  \end{enumerate}
  
\end{enumerate}
