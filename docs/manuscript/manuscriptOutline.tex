\section*{Introduction}
% <><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><>
\begin{enumerate}

% Paragraph 1
\item Phenological shifts with climate change
  \begin{enumerate}
  
  \item Shifts in spring/autumn modify start and end of season
  
  \item Early spring + delayed autumn should increase growth
  
  \end{enumerate}
  
% Paragraph 2
\item Positive relationship between season length and growth is important
  \begin{enumerate}
  \item Carbon cycle models rely on this positive relationship
  
  \item This could impact climate solutions 
  
  \end{enumerate}

% Paragraph 3
\item Maybe longer GS don't increase growth
  \begin{enumerate}
  
  \item Dow showed longer GS didn't increase growth locally
  
  \item Cabon showed decoupling between GPP/Growth
  
  \end{enumerate}

% Paragraph 4
\item Longer GS does not necessarily mean more favorable conditions for growth throughout the season
  \begin{enumerate}
  
  \item Longer seasons are not necessarily warmer
  
  \item Earlier SOS, also means earlier EOS---gains cool early season days (little increase in GDD) but miss warmer late season days (Figure \ref{fig:GSfig})
  
  \item Water availability may constrain growth
  
  \item Competition may offset extra days to grow especially for below canopy trees
  
  \item Length and speed of growth is physiologically (or genetically?) controlled
  
  \item And how much phenology can track these changing conditions matters
  
  \end{enumerate}

% Paragraph 5
\item Leaf and wood phenology are not symmetrically plastic
  \begin{enumerate}
  
  \item Leaf phenology in the spring is very plastic (well studied)
  
  \item Leaf phenology and growth are synchronized = good proxy for growth SOS and EOS
  
  \item However, this does not say anything how much trees grow in a year
  
  \item How flexible growth is may change between species, but also across populations
  \end{enumerate}

% Paragraph 6 
\item Local adaptation to inform growth with climate change
  \begin{enumerate}
  
  \item Trees from northern provenance may grow less with longer seasons
  
  \item Budset is strongly locally adapted
  
  \item Common gardens are important for climate change and tree growth
  
  \end{enumerate}

% Paragraph 7
\item Tree growth will vary across species but will also depend on future environmental conditions   %emwJun26 I think this paragraph HERE needs to be a set up of the full intro two prepare for the next paragraph ... so you need a different topic and a couple more points and ... 
%crdJun29: not totally sure about the set up... I tried something.
  \begin{enumerate}
  \item To understand GS and growth relationship, need to look more precisely at when trees grow during the GS
  
  \item If growth is not plastic enough, trees may not be able to track favorable changing conditions in the season
  
  \item Varies across species and population, with the latter having a potential to inform how species will respond to future conditions
  
  \item Juvenile trees store little carbon, but are crucial for forest regeneration capacity
  
  \end{enumerate}
  

% Paragraph 8
\item Answer how GS and growth relate using juvenile trees in a common garden
  \begin{enumerate}
  
  \item Disentangle GS length vs GS thermal conditions
  
  \item Growth with tree rings, SOS and EOS with leaf phenology
  
  \item Calendar season: days between SOS and EOS and thermal season: GDD between these days
  
  \item Will look at different species from different provenances
  \end{enumerate}
\end{enumerate}

\caption{We show how shifting growing season length with climate change affects both the temperature trees experience throughout the growing season (a), and how much growing degree days (GDD) they accumulate (b). We used mean daily temperature data from the Boston airport Climate station for the smoothed temperature and GDD curves. We represent the changes in temperature before (1945-1965 in blue) and after the 1980s increase in climate warming (2005-2025 in red). With climate change, spring events (green arrow) have advanced and fall phenology (orange arrow) has delayed, but to much lesser extent (day advancements are fake and represented by the arrows), this lengthened the calendar growing season, but concurently changed the temperature trees experience early in their season. Growth onset earlier in the season means the first days of growth are cooler (a) resulting in little accumulation of GDD (b). Though shifts in EOS are weaker and the temperature experienced changed little (a), the GDD accumulation rate late in the season is higher than early in the season. Alongside warmer summer temperature, this results in higher GDD accumulation, warming their thermal seasons.}
\label{fig:GSfig}
\end{figure}

\section*{Discussion}

\begin{enumerate}
% Section ----------------------------------------------------------------------
\item 
  \begin{enumerate}
  \item Used 81 juvenile trees of 4 species from 4 provenances to understand if longer GS increased   growth 
    \begin{enumerate}
    \item We used annual growth with tree rings and leaf phenology for SOS and EOS
    
    \item Longer GS increased growth for all species, but varied depending on the metric 
    
    \item Thermal season was a better predictor for growth than the calendar season
    
    \item Little effect of provenance
    \end{enumerate}
  \end{enumerate}

% Section ----------------------------------------------------------------------
\item The thermal season better predicts growth than the calendar season
  % Paragraph 2
  \begin{enumerate}
  \item Thermal is a better predictor of growth because it's more closely aligned to when and how fast trees grow
    \begin{enumerate}
    
    \item Thermal weights growth days better than calendar season
    
    \item Cool spring days count for less for thermal season vs calendar season (Figure \ref{fig:GSfig})
    
    \item Stronger response to EOS than SOS, because temperature is warmer = more GDD (Figure \ref{fig:GSfig}) (Buonaiuto, unpublished)
    
    \end{enumerate}
  
  % Paragraph 3
  \item SOS and EOS to disentangle the calendar season effect on growth
    \begin{enumerate}
    
    \item Early SOS didn't increase growth for most species (like Dow 2022)
    
    \item Studies that solely focus on SOS will miss that longer seasons may lead to more growth
    
    \item Late EOS increased growth for most species; relates to GDD, less to GSL
    
    \end{enumerate}
  

  % Paragraph 4
  \item Future response of growth in response to soil moisture is uncertain
    \begin{enumerate}
    \item Later EOS coupled with appropriate soil moisture $=$ growth increase---as we and others found
    
    \item No current increased drought in our species native range $=$ growth would increase
    
    \item Positive effect of later EOS offset if soil moisture becomes inadequate %emwJun26 and here you could discuss the one late season drought year a little perhaps? I suggest a quick lit search on whether late season droughts are expected to increase of have increased in the NE of NA with ACC. 
    
    \item While 2020 had a late of season drought, late of season droughts in the north east are rate---could change with warmer conditions
    \item Other temperate regions have growth decrease attributed to reduced soil moisture
    
    \end{enumerate}
  \end{enumerate}
  
% Section ----------------------------------------------------------------------
\item Contrasting growth responses across species, but not across provenances
  \begin{enumerate}
  \item Species responded differently to different season predictors
    \begin{enumerate}
    
    \item {\Ainc} grow more with GSL and earlier SOS: most growth happens in spring
    
    \item {\Ainc} is ruderal, takes advantage of cool and short days in spring
    
    \item Contrary, {\Bpap} and {\Bpop} driven by EOS: more reactive to warmer, late season temperature
    
    \item {\Ball} SOS and EOS trends counterinteract = no response to GSL
    
    \end{enumerate}
  
  \item Growth strategies across species may change how responsive they are
    \begin{enumerate}
    \item All our species are either fast or moderately fast growing species---known to track changing conditions better
    
    \item This could change if we used slow growing species
    
    \item Juvenile stages are known to be more reactive, could explain why they grew more with warmer seasons
    
    \item These trees in nature would be below canopy, so they would have much less light and might not have responded positively
    \end{enumerate}
  
  \item No effect of provenance suggests local adaption effect on growth is weak under current conditions
    \begin{enumerate}
    \item Good understanding of which leaf phenophase is locally adapted: budset $>$ leafout
    
    \item Little evidence of local adaptation on growth may be caused by little local adaptation on budset
    
    \item We know little about the effect of local adaptation on growth
    
    \item Understanding this requires teasing out the effects of local adaptation on leaf and wood phenology at the SOS and EOS
    \end{enumerate}
  \end{enumerate}

% Section ----------------------------------------------------------------------
\item Future carbon models and range shift projections need to consider species 

  \begin{enumerate}
  % Paragraph
  \item What we did is important 
    \begin{enumerate}
  
    \item Juvenile trees may be more responsive, but they would be competing for light in nature
    
    \item EOS and SOS asymmetrically matter to growth and we don't understand why
    
    \item Highlights importance of considering not just SOS (e.g. Dow, Wheeler2016Snow)
  
    \item Closely related species trends differed; they matter and we don't consider them enough
    
    \end{enumerate}

  \end{enumerate}
\end{enumerate}
