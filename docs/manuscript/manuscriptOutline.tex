\section*{Introduction}
% <><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><>
\begin{enumerate}

% Paragraph 1
\item Phenological shifts with climate change
  \begin{enumerate}
  
  \item Shifts in spring/autumn modify start and end of season
  
  \item Early spring + delayed autumn should increase growth
  
  \item This is important because carbon cycle models rely on this positive relationship
  
  \end{enumerate}

% Paragraph 2
\item Maybe longer GS don't increase growth
  \begin{enumerate}
  
  \item Dow showed longer GS didn't increase growth locally
  
  \item Cabon showed decoupling between GPP/Growth
  
  \end{enumerate}

% Paragraph 3
\item Longer GS does not necessarily mean better conditions throughout the season
  \begin{enumerate}
  
  \item Longer seasons are not necessarily warmer
  
  \item Early SOS does not increase GDD by much (Figure \ref{fig:GSfig})
  
  \item Water availability may constrain growth
  
  \item Competition may offset extra days to grow especially for below canopy trees
  
  \end{enumerate}

% Paragraph 4
\item Even with good conditions, trees may be internally constrained
  \begin{enumerate}
  
  \item Leaf phenology may be more plastic than growth
  
  \item How plastic growth is depends on species and provenance
  
  \end{enumerate}

% Paragraph 5
\item Local adaptation to inform growth with climate change
  \begin{enumerate}
  
  \item Trees from northern provenance may grow less with longer seasons
  
  \item Budset is strongly locally adapted
  
  \item Common gardens are important for climate change and tree growth
  
  \end{enumerate}

% Paragraph 6
\item Answer how GS and growth relate using juvenile trees in a common garden
  \begin{enumerate}
  
  \item Disentangle GS length vs GS thermal conditions
  
  \item Growth with tree rings, SOS and EOS with leaf phenology
  
  \item Calendar season: days between SOS and EOS and thermal season: GDD between these days
  
  \item Will look at different species from different provenances
  \end{enumerate}
\end{enumerate}

% Conceptual figure
\begin{figure}[!ht]
\includegraphics[width=0.9\textwidth]{../../analyses/figures/climate/gsconceptualfig.jpeg}
\caption{We show how shifting growing season length with climate change affects both the temperature trees experience during the growing season (a), and how much growing degree days (GDD) they accumulate (b). We used mean daily temperature data from the Boston airport Climate station for the smoothed temperature and GDD curves. We represent the changes in temperature before (1945-1965 in blue) and after the 1980s increase in climate warming (2005-2025 in red). With climate change, spring events (green arrow) have advanced and fall phenology (orange arrow) has delayed, but to much lesser extent (day advancements are fake and represented by the arrows), this lengthened the calendar growing season, but concurently changed the temperature trees experience early in their season. Growth onset earlier in the season means the first days of growth are cooler (a) resulting in little accumulation of GDD (b). Though shifts in EOS are weaker and the temperature experienced changed little (a), the GDD accumulation rate late in the season is higher than early in the season. Alongside warmer summer temperature, this results in higher GDD accumulation, warming their thermal seasons.}
\label{fig:GSfig}
\end{figure}

\section*{Results}
\begin{enumerate}
\item Does tree growth increase with longer growing seasons?
\item Did an earlier start and later end of season increase growth? 
\item Does tree growth increase with longer growing seasons and is it consistent across species and provenances?
\item How did growth change across years, and how did it relate to climate? \\
\end{enumerate}

\begin{enumerate}
\item Summary basic information
  \begin{enumerate}
  
  \item Wildchrokie includes four deciduous tree species from four sites with leaf phenology data spaning three years (2018 to 2020) for a total of 239 leafout and 
  230 budset observations, yielding 
  227 complete growing seasons (GS) for 
  81 individuals. 
    
  \item Mean summer temperature were similar across years with 2019 being the coolest (22.38$^\circ$C), 2020 the hottest (23.07$^\circ$C), and 2018 in between (22.85$^\circ$C)
    
  \item The calendar and thermal seasons ranged from 
  90 days and 
  1465.1 GDD ({\Ball} in 2018) to 
  173 days and 2513.6 GDD ({\Ainc} in 2018)

  \item The earliest leafout day of year was 
  22-Apr ({\Ainc} in
  2019) and the latest,
  05-Jun (also {\Ainc}, but in
  2020). 
  The earliest budset day of year was 
  13-Aug ({\Ball} in
  2018) and the latest, 
  24-Oct ({\Ainc} in
  2018) 
  
  \item No drought for the three years, except for in 2020, where a moderate summer drought ramped up to a severe drought in September.
  
  \item Annual ring widths across years varied from 
  0.39 mm ({\Ball} in 2020) to 
  13.55 mm ({\Bpop} in 2018) (Figure \ref{fig:wcboxplot}). 
 
\end{enumerate}

% Question 1 --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- ---
\item Does tree growth increase with longer growing seasons?
  \begin{enumerate}

  \item Longer growing seasons increased growth for all species, but varied depending on the metric used to measure it.

  \item Longer thermal season increased growth for {\Bpap} 
  ($\beta = $ 
  0.44, $UI_{90}$
  [0.18, 
  0.7]),
  {\Bpop} 
  ($\beta = $ 
  0.22, $UI_{90}$
  [0.07, 
  0.36]), and {\Ball} 
  ($\beta = $ 
  0.13, $UI_{90}$
  [0.01, 
  0.24]), but {\Ainc} had a weak effect, but trended in the same direction 
  ($\beta = $ 
  0.07, $UI_{90}$
  [-0.04, 
  0.17]) (Figure \ref{fig:wcmuplotbspp}a and e) (Table\ref{tab:wc_slope_table}).
 
  \item {\Ainc} grew more with longer calendar seasons ($\beta = $ 
  0.05, $UI_{90}$
  [0, 
  0.1]), but of the three species that responded to thermal season, only {\Bpap} also grew more with longer calendar seasons ($\beta = $ 
  0.18, $UI_{90}$
  [0.05, 
  0.32]).
  \item {\Ball} ($\beta = $ 
  0.03, $UI_{90}$
  [-0.02, 
  0.09]), and {\Bpop} ($\beta = $ 
  0.05, $UI_{90}$
  [-0.02, 
  0.13]) trended in the same direction (Figure \ref{fig:wcmuplotbspp}b and f) (Table \ref{tab:wc_slope_table}).
  
  \item Results with basal area increment (BAI) for the GDD model were broadly similar to ring width (see Table \ref{tab:BAItab}) 
  
  \item On a scaled axis, both predictors were similarly predictive (Figure\ref{fig:bsppZscored}). 
  \end{enumerate}
  
% Question 2 --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- ---
\item Did a later start and end of season increase growth?
  \begin{enumerate}
  \item A longer calendar season increased growth for two species, but only one grew more with an earlier start of season ({\Ainc}: $\beta = $ 
  -0.07, $UI_{90}$
  [-0.14, 
  -0.01]) (Figure \ref{fig:wcmuplotbspp}c and g) (Table \ref{tab:wc_slope_table}).
  
  \item {\Ball} which did not grow more with longer calendar season, grew less with an earlier start of season, unlike our prediction ($\beta = $ 
  0.1, $UI_{90}$
  [0, 
  0.2]) (Figure \ref{fig:wcmuplotbspp}c and g) (Table \ref{tab:wc_slope_table}).
  
  \item Three species grew more with a later end of season ({\Ball}: $\beta = $ 
  0.12, $UI_{90}$
  [0.05, 
  0.18], 
  {\Bpop}: $\beta = $ 
  0.1, $UI_{90}$
  [0.02, 
  0.17]), with {\Bpap} 
  ($\beta = $ 
  0.21, $UI_{90}$
  [0.09, 
  0.33])
  being the only one of those three that also grew more with longer calendar season (Figure \ref{fig:wcmuplotbspp}d and h) (Table \ref{tab:wc_slope_table}).
  
  \item The estimates were unchanged when using the full (SOS $n =$ 239 and $n =$ 230) or restricted datasets ($n=$227) (Figure \ref{fig:wcSOSEOScomparison}a). 
  
  \end{enumerate}

% Question 3 --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- ---
\item How did tree growth change across years, provenances and tree ids?
  \begin{enumerate}
  
  \item We did not find any clear effect of year on growth (Figure \ref{fig:wcmuplotayear}) and there is no clear differences of growth looking across species (Figure \ref{fig:wcxyGDD} and Figure \ref{fig:wcxyGSL})
  
  \item Growth did not change across any provenances (Figure \ref{fig:wcmuplotasite}) (Table \ref{tab:wc_asite_table}), and the variation across sites ($\sigma = $
  0.18, $UI_{90}$
  [0.02, 
  0.5) and tree ids ($\sigma = $ 
  0.17, $UI_{90}$
  [0.05, 
  0.27]) was similar

  \end{enumerate}


\end{enumerate}

\section*{Discussion}

\begin{enumerate}
\item Used 81 juvenile trees of 4 species from 4 provenances to understand if longer GS increased growth 
  \begin{enumerate}
  \item We used annual growth with tree rings and leaf phenology for SOS and EOS
  
  \item Longer GS increased growth for all species, but varied depending on the metric 
  
  \item Thermal season was a better predictor for growth than the calendar season
  
  \item SOS and EOS trend direction changed across species
  
  \item Little effect of provenance
  \end{enumerate}

% Section A --------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection*{A. Overall patterns}
% Paragraph
\item Why thermal season better predicts growth than the calendar season?
  \begin{enumerate}
  
  \item Thermal weights growth days better than calendar season
  
  \item Cool spring days count for less for thermal season vs calendar season (Figure \ref{fig:GSfig})
  
  \item Stronger response to EOS than SOS, because temperature is warmer = more GDD (Figure \ref{fig:GSfig})
  
  \end{enumerate}
  
% Paragraph
\item SOS and EOS to disentangle the calendar season effect on growth
  \begin{enumerate}
  
  \item Early SOS didn't increase growth for most species (like Dow 2022)
  
  \item Late EOS increased growth for most species; relates to GDD, less to GSL
  
  \end{enumerate}

% Section B --------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection*{B. Species specific patterns}
\item Certain species
  \begin{enumerate}
  
  \item {\Ainc} grow more with GSL and earlier SOS: most growth happens in spring
  
  \item {\Ainc} is ruderal, takes advantage of cool and short days in spring
  
  \item Contrary, {\Bpap} and {\Bpop} driven by EOS: more reactive to warmer, late season temperature
  
  \item {\Ball} SOS and EOS trends counterinteract = no response to GSL
  
  \end{enumerate}

\item No effect of provenance
  \begin{enumerate}
  \item Local adaption effect on growth is weak under current conditions
  
  \item Little effect on budset too (Buonaiuto, unpublished)
  \end{enumerate}

% Section C --------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection*{C. Future directions, limitation and conclusion}
% Paragraph
\item Limitations
  \begin{enumerate}
  \item No extreme temperatures in models
  
  \item Low sample size and not a fully crossed study design
  
  \item Our trees would be below canopy in nature = light competition
  \end{enumerate}

% Paragraph
\item What we did is important
  \begin{enumerate}

  \item Species trends differed, even if closely related
  
  \item Carbon models that pool across species is a weakness
  \end{enumerate}

\end{enumerate}
