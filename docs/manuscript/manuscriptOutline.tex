\begin{enumerate}

% Paragraph 1
\item Phenological shifts with climate change
  \begin{enumerate}
  
  \item Shifts in spring/autumn modify start and end of season
  
  \item Early spring + delayed autumn should increase growth
  
  \end{enumerate}
  
% Paragraph 2
\item Positive relationship between season length and growth is important
  \begin{enumerate}
  \item Carbon cycle models rely on this positive relationship
  
  \item This could impact climate solutions %emw22Jun -- oh la la -- nice one!
  
  \end{enumerate}

% Paragraph 3
\item Maybe longer GS don't increase growth
  \begin{enumerate}
  
  \item Dow showed longer GS didn't increase growth locally
  
  \item Cabon showed decoupling between GPP/Growth
  
  \end{enumerate}

% Paragraph 4
\item Longer GS does not necessarily mean more favorable conditions for growth throughout the season
  \begin{enumerate}
  
  \item Longer seasons are not necessarily warmer
  
  \item Early SOS does not increase GDD by much (Figure \ref{fig:GSfig})
  
  \item Water availability may constrain growth
  
  \item Competition may offset extra days to grow especially for below canopy trees
  
  \end{enumerate}

% Paragraph 5
\item Different plant stages (leaves vs wood) differ in their plasticity
  \begin{enumerate}
  
  \item Leaf phenology is very plastic (well studied)
  
  \item Leaf phenology and growth are synchronized = good proxy for growth SOS and EOS
  
  \item However, it this does not say anything how much trees grow in a year %emw22Jun -- not 100% sure what you mean here ... be care to be TOO detailed (`annual growth increment' is pretty specific for the reader at this point) too fast.
  
  \end{enumerate}

% Paragraph 6 %emw22Jun -- I wonder if this belongs after paragraph 5? It may be easier for the reader if you keep leaf phenology = plastic (or not) together %crd25Jun: changed for after paragraph 5
\item Local adaptation to inform growth with climate change
  \begin{enumerate}
  
  \item Trees from northern provenance may grow less with longer seasons
  
  \item Budset is strongly locally adapted
  
  \item Common gardens are important for climate change and tree growth
  
  \end{enumerate}

% Paragraph 7
\item Even with good conditions, trees may not be able to respond to longer GS
  \begin{enumerate}
  \item Earlier SOS, also means earlier EOS---gains cool early season days but miss warmer late season days 
  
  \item Tree growth rate cannot increase indefinitely
  
  \item Length and speed of growth is physiologically (or genetically?) controlled
  
  \end{enumerate}
  

% Paragraph 8
\item Answer how GS and growth relate using juvenile trees in a common garden
  \begin{enumerate}
  
  \item Disentangle GS length vs GS thermal conditions
  
  \item Growth with tree rings, SOS and EOS with leaf phenology
  
  \item Calendar season: days between SOS and EOS and thermal season: GDD between these days
  
  \item Will look at different species from different provenances
  \end{enumerate}
\end{enumerate}

\caption{We show how shifting growing season length with climate change affects both the temperature trees experience throughout the growing season (a), and how much growing degree days (GDD) they accumulate (b). We used mean daily temperature data from the Boston airport Climate station for the smoothed temperature and GDD curves. We represent the changes in temperature before (1945-1965 in blue) and after the 1980s increase in climate warming (2005-2025 in red). With climate change, spring events (green arrow) have advanced and fall phenology (orange arrow) has delayed, but to much lesser extent (day advancements are fake and represented by the arrows), this lengthened the calendar growing season, but concurently changed the temperature trees experience early in their season. Growth onset earlier in the season means the first days of growth are cooler (a) resulting in little accumulation of GDD (b). Though shifts in EOS are weaker and the temperature experienced changed little (a), the GDD accumulation rate late in the season is higher than early in the season. Alongside warmer summer temperature, this results in higher GDD accumulation, warming their thermal seasons.}
\label{fig:GSfig}
\end{figure}

\begin{enumerate}
\item Summary basic information
  \begin{enumerate}
  
  \item Wildchrokie includes four deciduous tree species from four sites with leaf phenology data spaning three years (2018 to 2020) for a total of 239 leafout and 
  230 budset observations, yielding 
  227 complete growing seasons (GS) for 
  81 individuals. 
    
  \item Mean summer temperature were similar across years with 2019 being the coolest (22.38$^\circ$C), 2020 the hottest (23.07$^\circ$C), and 2018 in between (22.85$^\circ$C)
    
  \item The calendar and thermal seasons ranged from 
  90 days and 
  1465.1 GDD ({\Ball} in 2018) to 
  173 days and 2513.6 GDD ({\Ainc} in 2018)

  \item The earliest leafout day of year was 
  22-Apr ({\Ainc} in
  2019) and the latest,
  05-Jun (also {\Ainc}, but in
  2020). 
  The earliest budset day of year was 
  13-Aug ({\Ball} in
  2018) and the latest, 
  24-Oct ({\Ainc} in
  2018) 
  
  \item No drought for the three years, except for in 2020, where a moderate summer drought ramped up to a severe drought in September.
  
  \item Annual ring widths across years varied from 
  0.39 mm ({\Ball} in 2020) to 
  13.55 mm ({\Bpop} in 2018) (Figure \ref{fig:wcboxplot}). 
 
\end{enumerate}

\section*{Discussion}

\begin{enumerate}
% Section ----------------------------------------------------------------------
\item 
  \begin{enumerate}
  \item Used 81 juvenile trees of 4 species from 4 provenances to understand if longer GS increased   growth 
    \begin{enumerate}
    \item We used annual growth with tree rings and leaf phenology for SOS and EOS
    
    \item Longer GS increased growth for all species, but varied depending on the metric 
    
    \item Thermal season was a better predictor for growth than the calendar season
    
    \item Little effect of provenance
    \end{enumerate}
  \end{enumerate}

% Section ----------------------------------------------------------------------
\item The thermal season better predicts growth than the calendar season
  % Paragraph
  \begin{enumerate}
  \item Thermal is a better predictor of growth because it's more closely aligned to when and how fast trees grow
    \begin{enumerate}
    
    \item Thermal weights growth days better than calendar season
    
    \item Cool spring days count for less for thermal season vs calendar season (Figure \ref{fig:GSfig})
    
    \item Stronger response to EOS than SOS, because temperature is warmer = more GDD (Figure \ref{fig:GSfig}) (Buonaiuto, unpublished)
    
    \end{enumerate}
  
  % Paragraph
  \item SOS and EOS to disentangle the calendar season effect on growth
    \begin{enumerate}
    
    \item Early SOS didn't increase growth for most species (like Dow 2022) %emw22Jun -- and ... what does that mean? I would spell out here or somewhere that this could mean that studies that focus on just SOS will miss that longer seasons may lead to more growth; this is a very important point!
    
    \item Late EOS increased growth for most species; relates to GDD, less to GSL
    
    \item ACC and decreasing water availability means that importance of EOS could harm tree growth in nature %emw22Jun --hmm, okay -- but this sounds to me the usual knee-jerk, let me think of a way this could be BAD!     \end{enumerate}
  \end{enumerate}

% Section ----------------------------------------------------------------------
\item Contrasting growth responses across species, but not across provenances %emw22Jun -- cool stuff here. 
  \begin{enumerate}
  \item Species responded differently to different season predictors
    \begin{enumerate}
    
    \item {\Ainc} grow more with GSL and earlier SOS: most growth happens in spring
    
    \item {\Ainc} is ruderal, takes advantage of cool and short days in spring
    
    \item Contrary, {\Bpap} and {\Bpop} driven by EOS: more reactive to warmer, late season temperature
    
    \item {\Ball} SOS and EOS trends counterinteract = no response to GSL

    \item Local adaption effect on growth is weak under current conditions %emw22Jun -- I think you could riff a little more here -- just perhaps one more sentence (or more) on how your results suggest looking for local adaptation on growth may require teasing out the effects of local adaptation on EOS and SOS (for leaf phenology and also for wood phenology)

  \end{enumerate}
