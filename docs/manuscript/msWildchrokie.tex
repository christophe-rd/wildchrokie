\section*{Methods}

%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
% Common garden setup
%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection*{Common garden setup}
We collected seeds from 18 species of deciduous trees and shrubs from four provenances across a 3.5$^{\circ}$ latitudinal gradient (Figure \ref{fig:mapProv} and Table \ref{tab:provs}) in 2014-2015, germinated them following standard germination protocols and grew them to seedlings \citep{baskin_seeds_2014}. In the spring of 2017, we planted them outside in the garden located at the Weld Hill Research Building at the Arnold Arboretum in Boston MA (42.30\degree N, 71.13\degree W). Plots were regularly weeded and watered throughout the duration of the study and were pruned in the fall of 2020.

%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
% Phenological monitoring and sample collection
%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection*{Phenological monitoring and sample collection}
For the years 2018-2020, we monitored leaf phenology for all individuals in the common garden once or twice per week from February to December (Figure \ref{fig:phenoTimeSeries}). See (Buonuito, unpublished) for more details about the phenological monitoring. In the spring of 2023, we collected tree ring samples only for the tree species with high survivorship. Thus, we have four species in the \textit{Betulaceae} family. We collected cross-sections on 78 individuals by cutting the trunk above the root collar, and cores on 43 individuals using standard dendrochonological methods \citep{cook_methods_1990}, for a total of 81 individuals (Table \ref{tab:provs}). We stored the cores in modified plastic straws to allow aeration and were left to dry with the cross-sections at ambient temperature for three months. 

%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
% Sample processing, imaging and measuring
%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection*{Sample processing, imaging and measuring}
We mounted the cores on wooden mounts, and sanded both the cores and cross-sections using progressively finer sandpaper grits (150, 300, 400, 600, 800, 1000), then scanned the cores and cross-sections at a resolution of 6250 dpi with a high-resolution treering scanner (Fong, unpublished). Using the digitized images, we measured  ring widths with ImageJ \citep{schneider_nih_2012} at three standard locations for each cross-section (at {120\degree} from each other, averaging the three measurements to yield one ring width per year, per individual), and one ring width for each core. When we had cross-sections and cores for an invidividual, we used only the measurement from the cross sections. \\
\par
We performed visual cross dating. We did not perform statistical crossdating because of the short chronologies that limit the capacity of these analyses \citep{raden_potential_2020}, and for similar reasons, we did not detrend the ring widths. Visual analyses of trends showed no consistent allometric patterns (Figure \ref{fig:allom}); thus we included only a year intercept to correct for potential effects of different years, not otherwise included in our model. \\

\subsection*{Data analyses}
To understand how growth shifted with season lengths we used linear Bayesian hierarchical models, estimating ring width with differing season lengths using four different season predictors (GDD, GSL, SOS and EOS). We calculated the thermal season using growing degree days (GDD) (mean daily temperature above 5\degree C) accumulated between leafout and budset, and the calendar season (or growing season length (GSL)) as the number of days between leafout and budset. Finally, we used leafout and budset as the start (SOS) and end of the season (EOS) as separate predictors that represent the boundaries of the growing season. \\

We used the daily climate data from the climate station located at Weldhill research station for 2018 and 2019 (\url{https://arboretum.harvard.edu/research/data-resources/weather/}). Because the data was not available for that station after August 2020, we used the daily climate data from the weather station “Boston (Weld Hill)" of the Network for Environment and Weather Applications (NEWA) for the whole year in 2020 (\url{https://newa.cornell.edu/all-weather-data-query/}). \\

For each predictor, we estimated its effect on ring width observed for each tree in each year as:

\[
\begin{aligned} 
ln(ringwidth)_i &\sim N(\mu, \sigma_y) \\
\mu &= \alpha + \alpha_{\text{species}} + \alpha_{\text{prov}} + \alpha_{\text{treeid}} + \alpha_{\text{year}} + \beta_{species} \times \text{season predictor} \\
\alpha_{\text{prov}} &\sim N(0, \sigma_{\alpha \, \text{prov}}) \\
\alpha_{\text{treeid}} &\sim N(0, \sigma_{\alpha \, \text{treeid}}) \\
\end{aligned}
\]

where $i$ denotes each observation, $\alpha$ represent intercepts and $\beta$ represent slopes. This model yields a species-specific effect of each seasonal predictor (e.g. GDD) while also allowing other variation due to species ($\alpha_{species}$), alongside variation due to provenance ($\alpha_{prov}$), tree id ($\alpha_{treeid}$), year ($\alpha_{year}$) and a species specific growth rate ($\beta_{species}$). We chose weakly informative priors for all models; increasing the priors by 2x did not change the results trends. We ran four chains for a total of 8000 warmup (discarded) and 8000 sampling iterations, which we kept for posterior distribution estimates. The models did not have any divergent transitions, had a $\hat{R}$ below 1.01, and values of effective sample size ($n_{\text{eff}}$) greater than 10\% of the model iterations. We wrote our models using Stan \citep{carpenter_stan_2017} with the rstan package version 2.32.7 \citep{stan_development_team_rstan_2025} to run the Stan code in R. \\

\par
As a supplementary analyses, we ran the models using basal area increment (BAI), as the response variable. For this, we summed each ring width separately at the three distinct locations on the cross sections yielding three radii from pit to bark. We then calculated the area of each ring by subtracting the radius from the inner ring from the outer ring: $\pi \times (\text{Inner radius}^2 - \text{Outer radius}^2)$. Then, we averaged across the three BAI for each ring and fit the models with the four different predictors. \\

We also fit the models for SOS and EOS using the full vs restricted datasets.

\subsection*{Summary basic information}
Wildchrokie includes four deciduous tree species from four sites with leaf phenology data spanning three years (2018 to 2020) for a total of 239 leafout and 
230 budset observations, yielding 
227 complete growing seasons (GS) for 
81 individuals. At the common garden location, the mean summer temperature was similar across years with 2019 being the coolest 
(22.38$^\circ$C), 2020 the hottest 
(23.07$^\circ$C), and 2018 in between 
(22.85$^\circ$C). There was no drought for those three years, except for in 2020, where a moderate summer drought ramped up to a severe drought in September. The calendar and thermal seasons ranged from 
90 days and 
1465.1 GDD ({\Ball} in 2018) to 
173 days and 2513.6 GDD ({\Ainc} in 2018). The earliest leafout day of year was 
22-Apr ({\Ainc} in
2019) and the latest,
05-Jun (also {\Ainc}, but in
2020). The earliest budset day of year was 
13-Aug ({\Ball} in
2018) and the latest, 
24-Oct ({\Ainc} in
2018). Finally, annual ring widths across years varied from 
0.39 mm ({\Ball} in 2020) to 
13.55 mm ({\Bpop} in 2018) (Figure \ref{fig:wcmuplotayear}). 

%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
% Question 1
%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection*{Does tree growth increase with longer growing seasons?}
Longer growing seasons increased growth for all species, but varied depending on the metric used to measure it. Indeed, warmer thermal season increased growth for {\Bpap} ($\beta = $ 
0.44, $UI_{90}$
[0.18, 
0.7]), {\Bpop} ($\beta = $ 
0.22, $UI_{90}$
[0.07, 
0.36]), and {\Ball} ($\beta = $ 
0.13, $UI_{90}$
[0.01, 
0.24]), but {\Ainc} trended in the same direction ($\beta = $ 
0.07, $UI_{90}$
[-0.04, 
0.17]) (Figure \ref{fig:wcmuplotbspp}a and e) (Table\ref{tab:wc_slope_table}). \\
 
Unlike its response to warmer thermal season, {\Ainc} grew more with longer calendar seasons ($\beta = $ 
0.05, $UI_{90}$
[0, 
0.1]). However, of the three species that responded to thermal season, only {\Bpap} also grew more with longer calendar seasons ($\beta = $ 
0.18, $UI_{90}$
[0.05, 
0.32]), but {\Ball} ($\beta = $ 
0.03, $UI_{90}$
[-0.02, 
0.09]), and {\Bpop} ($\beta = $ 
0.05, $UI_{90}$
[-0.02, 
0.13]) trended in the same direction (Figure \ref{fig:wcmuplotbspp}b and f) (Table \ref{tab:wc_slope_table}). \\
  
On a scaled axis, both predictors were similarly predictive (Figure \ref{fig:bsppZscored}). Additionally, the results with basal area increment (BAI) for the GDD model were broadly similar to ring width (see Table \ref{tab:BAItab}). 

%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
% Question 2
%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection*{Did a later start and end of season increase growth?}
A longer calendar season increased growth for two species, but only one also grew more with an earlier start of season ({\Ainc}: $\beta = $ 
-0.07, $UI_{90}$
[-0.14, 
-0.01]) (Figure \ref{fig:wcmuplotbspp}c and g) (Table \ref{tab:wc_slope_table}). However, {\Ball} which did not grow more with longer calendar season, grew less with an earlier start of season---unlike what we expected ($\beta = $ 
0.1, $UI_{90}$
[0, 
0.2]) (Figure \ref{fig:wcmuplotbspp}c and g) (Table \ref{tab:wc_slope_table})---but more with a later end of season ($\beta = $ 
0.12, $UI_{90}$
[0.05, 
0.18]). Alongside {\Ball}, two other species grew more with a later end of season ({\Bpop}: $\beta = $ 
0.1, $UI_{90}$
[0.02, 
0.17], and {\Bpap}:$\beta = $ 
0.21, $UI_{90}$
[0.09, 
0.33]), with the later being the only one of those three that also grew more with longer calendar season (Figure \ref{fig:wcmuplotbspp}d and h) (Table \ref{tab:wc_slope_table}). \\
  
The estimates were unchanged when using the full (SOS $n =$ 
239 and $n =$ 
230) or restricted datasets ($n=$ 
227) (Figure \ref{fig:wcSOSEOScomparison}a). 
  
%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
% Question 3
%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection*{How did tree growth change across years, provenances and tree ids?}
We did not find any clear effect of year on growth (Figure \ref{fig:wcmuplotayear}) and there is no apparent differences of growth looking across species (Figure \ref{fig:wcxyGDD} and \ref{fig:wcxyGSL}). Likewise, growth did not change across any provenances (Figure \ref{fig:wcmuplotasite}) (Table \ref{tab:wc_asite_table}), and the variation across provenances ($\sigma = $
0.18, $UI_{90}$
[0.02, 
0.5) and tree ids ($\sigma = $ 
0.17, $UI_{90}$
[0.05, 
0.27]) was similar.
