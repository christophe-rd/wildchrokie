\subsection{Growing season length and conditions}
%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
\begin{enumerate}

% GSL length
\item Across the three years and all species, the growing season length varied from 90 days to 173 days. 

% GSL length
\item The year with longest growing season length on average was 
2019 with 141.49 days,
and the shortest was 
2020 with 135.87 days.

% Temperature spring
\item The hottest spring period of the three years (MAM) was
2020 with a mean mean temperature of 
9.05$^\circ$C,
and the coldest,
2019 with a mean mean temperature of
8.72$^\circ$C.

% Temperature summer
\item The hottest summer period of the three years (JJA) was
2020 with a mean temperature of 23.07$^\circ$C,
and the coldest,
2019 with a mean temperature of 22.38$^\circ$C.

% PDSI spring
\item The driest spring period of the three years (MAM) was
2019 with a PDSI value of 2.74,
and the wettest,
2020 with a PDSI value of 1.32.

% PDSI summer
\item The driest summer period (PDSI) of the three years (JJA) was
2019 with a PDSI value of 4.02,
and the wettest,
2020 with a PDSI value of -2.35.

% PPT spring
\item The spring period with the highest precipitation of the three years (MAM) was
2018 with a precipitation of 120.87,
and the lowest,
2020 with a precipitation of 91.77.

% PPT summer
\item The summer period with the highest precipitation of the three years (JJA) was
2019 with a precipitation of 115.33,
and the lowest,
2020 with a precipitation of 66.37.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Model results}
%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Our results show similar trends in response to the thermal (GDD) versus the calendar growing season (GSL), with a slightly stronger response to longer GSL compared to increased GDD. 

% Figure Growth X GDD
\begin{figure}[!ht]
\includegraphics[width=1\textwidth]{../../analyses/figures/empiricalData/growthModelSlopesperSppFacet.jpeg}
\caption{Ring width responses to growing degree days for the primary growing season. The shaded areas represent the 50\% confidence intervals. The dots represent the empirical data}
\label{fig:wcgrowthSlopes}
\end{figure}

\begin{enumerate}
% GDD
\item A. incana ($\beta = $ 0.001385, $UI_{90}$[\ensuremath{-4.05\times 10^{-4}}, 0.003215]),
B. alleghaniensis ($\beta = $ \ensuremath{8.75\times 10^{-4}}, $UI_{90}$[-0.00123, 0.00293]) and
B. papyrifera ($\beta = $ 0.00142, $UI_{90}$[-0.001365, 0.00423]) 
show a weak response, but seem to grow more with more GDD, while 
B. populifolia  ($\beta = $ \ensuremath{4.35\times 10^{-4}}, $UI_{90}$[-0.00175, 0.002615])  
did not respond to increased GDD 
(Figure \ref{fig:wcmuplotbspp}).

% Figure mu plot bspp GDD
\begin{figure}[!ht]
\includegraphics[width=0.8\textwidth]{../../analyses/figures/empiricalData/muALL.jpeg}
\caption{Model parameters across the different predictors}
\label{fig:wcmuplotbspp}
\end{figure}

% GSL
\item A. incana ($\beta = $ 0.0374, $UI_{90}$[0.0132, 0.0615]) is the only species for which its 90\% intervals don't overlap with zero suggesting that it grows more with longer growing season.
B. alleghaniensis ($\beta = $ 0.019, $UI_{90}$[-0.0095, 0.0477]) and
B. papyrifera ($\beta = $ 0.0327, $UI_{90}$[-0.0122, 0.0783]) 
show a weaker response, but seem to grow more in response to longer seasons, while 
B. populifolia  ($\beta = $ 0.0017, $UI_{90}$[-0.0336, 0.0354])  
did not respond to the GSL 
(Figure \ref{fig:wcmuplotbspp}).

% SOS
\item Ring width decreased with a later SOS for A. incana ($\beta = $ -0.0666, $UI_{90}$[-0.0899, -0.0428]) and B. populifolia ($\beta = $ -0.0451, $UI_{90}$[-0.0857, -0.0049]). Whereas 
B. alleghaniensis ($\beta = $ -0.02, $UI_{90}$[-0.0554, 0.0149]) and
B. papyrifera ($\beta = $ -0.0208, $UI_{90}$[-0.0682, 0.0275]) 
show a weaker response, but seem to grow less with later SOS.
(Figure \ref{fig:wcmuplotbspp}).

% EOS
\item Ring width decreased with a later EOS for A. incana ($\beta = $ -0.0272, $UI_{90}$[-0.0525, -0.0015]). B. populifolia showed a weaker response but appears to also grow less with a later EOS ($\beta = $ -0.0239, $UI_{90}$[-0.0542, 0.0058]). 
B. alleghaniensis ($\beta = $ 0.0046, $UI_{90}$[-0.0241, 0.0325]) and
B. papyrifera ($\beta = $ 0.0071, $UI_{90}$[-0.031, 0.0465]) 
didn't respond to EOS.
(Figure \ref{fig:wcmuplotbspp}).

% site
\item For the provenance with the GDD model, we detected a weak growth difference between 
G. College ($\alpha_{site} = $ -0.379, $UI_{90}$[-2.097, 1.299]), 
S. Hypolyte ($\alpha_{site} = $ -0.07, $UI_{90}$[-1.784, 1.635]) or 
W. Mountains ($\alpha_{site} = $ 0.471, $UI_{90}$[-1.208, 2.187]).
Only HF grew less ($\alpha_{site} = $ = -0.768, $UI_{90}$[-2.439, 0.936]) 
(Figure \ref{fig:wcmuplotbspp})



\end{enumerate}
