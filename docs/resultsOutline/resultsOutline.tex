%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Growing season length and conditions}
%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
\begin{enumerate}

% GSL length
\item Across the three years and all species, the growing season length varied from 90 days to 173 days. 

% GSL length
\item The year with longest growing season length on average was 
2019 with 141.49 days,
and the shortest was 
2020 with 135.87 days.

% Temperature spring
\item The hottest spring period of the three years (MAM) was
2020 with a mean mean temperature of 
9.05$^\circ$C,
and the coldest,
2019 with a mean mean temperature of
8.72$^\circ$C.

% Temperature summer
\item The hottest summer period of the three years (JJA) was
2020 with a mean temperature of 23.07$^\circ$C,
and the coldest,
2019 with a mean temperature of 22.38$^\circ$C.

% PDSI spring
\item The driest spring period of the three years (MAM) was
2020 with a PDSI value of 1.32,
and the wettest,
2019 with a PDSI value of 2.74.

% PDSI summer
\item The driest summer period (PDSI) of the three years (JJA) was
2020 with a PDSI value of -2.35,
and the wettest,
2019 with a PDSI value of 4.02.

% PPT spring
\item The spring period with the highest precipitation of the three years (MAM) was
2018 with a precipitation of 120.87,
and the lowest,
2020 with a precipitation of 91.77.

% PPT summer
\item The summer period with the highest precipitation of the three years (JJA) was
2019 with a precipitation of 115.33,
and the lowest,
2020 with a precipitation of 66.37.
\end{enumerate}

%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Phenology observations}
%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
\begin{enumerate}
\item Leafout dates across all years and species varied from 112 to 157 and budset date varied from 225 to 297.
\item On average, the earliest leafout date was in 2019 for all species (A. incana: 120.86, 
B alleghaniensis: 123, 
B. papyrifera: 123.33 and 
B. populifolia: 121. 
The latest leafout date for all species was in 2020 (
A. incana: 148.68, 
B alleghaniensis: 141.32, 
B. papyrifera: 136.57 and 
B. populifolia: 133.83).
No species was consistently the earliest nor the latest one to leafout.

\item On average, the earliest budset date was in 2019 for A. incana (
263.29) and 
B alleghaniensis (258), but in 2018 for
B. papyrifera (266.83) and 
B. populifolia (266.29). 
The latest budset date for all species was in 2020 (
A. incana: 280.88, 
B alleghaniensis: 264.05, 
B. papyrifera: 279.43 and 
B. populifolia: 282).
No species was consistently the earliest nor the latest set bud.

\end{enumerate}

%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Ring widths}
%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
\begin{enumerate}
\item The ring width of our species vary from 0.39mm to 13.55mm. 
\item Ring widths for A. incana varied from 0.61 to 7.81, 
from 0.39 to 6.92 for B. alleghaniensis, 
from 0.65 to 6.42 for B. papyrifera and 
from 0.75 to 13.55 for B. populifolia.
\end{enumerate}

%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Model results}
%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Our results show similar trends in response to the thermal (GDD) versus the calendar growing season (GSL), with a stronger response to longer GSL compared to increased GDD. Especially for A. incana not shifting its growth in response to GDD but showing a strong response to GSL, as well as the start and end of season.

% Figure mu across all species and climate predictors
\begin{figure}[!ht]
\includegraphics[width=0.8\textwidth]{../../analyses/figures/empiricalData/muALL.jpeg}
\caption{Model parameters across the different predictors}
\label{fig:wcmuplot}
\end{figure}

\begin{enumerate}

% GDD
\item A. incana ($\beta = $ 0.28, $UI_{90}$[-0.1, 0.65]),
B. alleghaniensis ($\beta = $ 0.18, $UI_{90}$[-0.24, 0.59]) and
B. papyrifera ($\beta = $ 0.29, $UI_{90}$[-0.27, 0.83]) 
show a weak response, but seem to grow more with more GDD, while 
B. populifolia  ($\beta = $ 0.08, $UI_{90}$[-0.36, 0.52])  
did not respond to increased GDD 
(Figure \ref{fig:wcmuplot}).

% Figure Growth X GDD
\begin{figure}[!ht]
\includegraphics[width=1\textwidth]{../../analyses/figures/empiricalData/growthModelSlopesperSppFacet.jpeg}
\caption{Ring width responses to growing degree days for the primary growing season. The shaded areas represent the 50\% confidence intervals. The dots represent the empirical data}
\label{fig:wcgrowthSlopesGDD}
\end{figure}


% GSL
\item A. incana ($\beta = $ 0.38, $UI_{90}$[0.14, 0.62]) is the only species for which its 90\% intervals don't overlap with zero suggesting that it grows more with longer growing season (GSL).
B. alleghaniensis ($\beta = $ 0.19, $UI_{90}$[-0.11, 0.5]) and
B. papyrifera ($\beta = $ 0.33, $UI_{90}$[-0.11, 0.78]) 
show a weaker response, but seem to grow more in response to longer seasons, while 
B. populifolia  ($\beta = $ 0.02, $UI_{90}$[-0.32, 0.36])  
did not respond to the GSL 
(Figure \ref{fig:wcmuplot}).

% Figure Growth X GSL
\begin{figure}[!ht]
\includegraphics[width=1\textwidth]{../../analyses/figures/empiricalData/growthModelSlopesperSppFacetGSL.jpeg}
\caption{Ring width responses to length of the primary growing season (days). The shaded areas represent the 50\% confidence intervals. The dots represent the empirical data}
\label{fig:wcgrowthSlopesGSL}
\end{figure}

% SOS
\item Ring width decreased with a later start of season (SOS) for 
A. incana ($\beta = $ -0.66, $UI_{90}$[-0.9, -0.43]) and 
B. populifolia ($\beta = $ -0.45, $UI_{90}$[-0.83, -0.05]). 
Whereas B. alleghaniensis ($\beta = $ -0.2, $UI_{90}$[-0.55, 0.14]) and
B. papyrifera ($\beta = $ -0.21, $UI_{90}$[-0.69, 0.27]) 
show a weaker response, but seem to grow less with later SOS.
(Figure \ref{fig:wcmuplot}).

% EOS
\item Ring width decreased with a later end of season (EOS) for A. incana ($\beta = $ -0.27, $UI_{90}$[-0.52, -0.02]). B. populifolia showed a weaker response but appears to also grow less with a later EOS ($\beta = $ -0.25, $UI_{90}$[-0.54, 0.05]). 
B. alleghaniensis ($\beta = $ 0.04, $UI_{90}$[-0.25, 0.33]) and
B. papyrifera ($\beta = $ 0.06, $UI_{90}$[-0.33, 0.45]) 
didn't respond to EOS.
(Figure \ref{fig:wcmuplot}).

% Site
\item For the provenance with the GDD model, the estimates didn't shift much across climate predictors. We detected a weak growth difference between 
G. College ($\alpha_{site} = $ -0.33, $UI_{90}$[-1.96, 1.27]), 
S. Hypolyte ($\alpha_{site} = $ -0.03, $UI_{90}$[-1.7, 1.6]) or 
W. Mountains ($\alpha_{site} = $ 0.51, $UI_{90}$[-1.17, 2.09]).
Only HF grew less ($\alpha_{site} = $ = -0.72, $UI_{90}$[-2.39, 0.92]) 
(Figure \ref{fig:wcmuplot})



\end{enumerate}
