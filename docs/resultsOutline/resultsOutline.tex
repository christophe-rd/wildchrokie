%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Growing season length and conditions}
%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
\begin{enumerate}

% GSL length and conditions
\item 2020 was the shortest season by days (mean GSL $=$135.9),
with an average summer temperature of 
23.1$^\circ$C,and a summer thermal sum of 
1644.6 GDD. \\
2019 was the longest season by days (mean GSL $=$
141.5)
with an average summer temperature of 
22.4$^\circ$C, and a summer thermal sum of 
1549.4 GDD. \\
2018 had an average GSL of 138.7 days, 
an average summer temperature of 
22.9$^\circ$, and summer thermal sum of 
1569.5 GDD

% SOS conditions
\item Leafout dates across all years and species varied from 
112 to 
157 and budset date varied from 
225 to 
297, which are the two phases that set the boundaries of the growing season.

\item 2019 had the earliest start of season (mean leafout date $=$
121.7), and the coldest spring of the three years
(8.7$^\circ$C), with a spring thermal sum of 
491.1 GDD. \\
2020 had the latest start of season (mean leafout date $=$
141), 
and was also the warmest spring (
9.1$^\circ$C), but had the lowest thermal sum of three years (
458.1 GDD). \\
2018 had an average start of season of 127.3,an average spring temperature of
8.9$^\circ$, and the highest spring thermal sum (
511 GDD).

% SOS by species 
\item On average, the earliest leafout date was in 2019 for all species (\Ainc: 120.9, 
B alleghaniensis: 123, 
\Bpap: 125.8 and 
\Bpop: 121.5). 
The latest leafout date for all species was in 2020 (
\Ainc: 149.1, 
B alleghaniensis: 141.3, 
\Bpap: 136.6 and 
\Bpop: 133.9).
No species was consistently the earliest nor the latest to leafout.

% EOS condition
\item 2019 had the earliest end of season (mean budset date $=$263.2), and was coldest autumn (average autumn temperature $=$
8.7$^\circ$C), with a autumn thermal sum of
491.1 GDD. \\
2020 had the latest end of season (mean budset date $=$276.8), and was the warmest autumn (average autumn temperature $=$
9.1$^\circ$C) with a autumn thermal sum of 
458.1 GDD. \\
2018 had an average end of season of 266, an average autumn temperature of 
8.9$^\circ$, 
and had the highest thermal sum of the three years (511 GDD).

% EOS by species 
\item On average, the earliest budset date was in 2019 for \Ainc (
263.3) and 
\Ball (258), but in 2018 for
\Bpap (266.8) and 
\Bpop (266.3). 
The latest budset date for all species was in 2020 (
\Ainc: 280.9, 
\Ball: 264.1, 
\Bpap: 279.4 and 
\Bpop: 282).
No species was consistently the earliest nor the latest to set buds.

% PDSI spring
\item The driest spring of the three years (MAM) (3-month averaged PDSI index) was
2020 with a PDSI value of 
1.3, a cumulative precipitation of 
275.3 mm.
2019 was the least dry spring with a PDSI value of 
2.7 and a cumulative precipitation of328.4 mm.

% PDSI summer
\item 2020 had the driest summer (JJA) with a PDSI value of 
-2.4, and a cumulative precipitation of 
199.1 mm.
2019 was the least dry summer with a PDSI value of 
4 and a cumulative precipitation of 
346 mm.

% Drought occurence
\item 2020 is the only year a with period of moderate drought ($PDSI [-3, -2]$) which was from 
June through 
August. Throughout the three years,
September of 
2020 was the only period with a severe drought ($PDSI < -3$) 

\end{enumerate}

%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Ring widths}
%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
\begin{enumerate}
\item The ring widths of our species vary from 0.4 mm to 13.5 mm. 
\item Ring widths for \Ball were the smallest and varied from 0.4 mm to 6.9 mm, and 
\Bpop had the largest rings which spanned from 
0.8 mm to 13.5 mm on average.
\Ainc (0.6 mm to 7.8 mm) and 
\Bpap were quite similar (0.7 mm to 6.4 mm).
\end{enumerate}

%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Model results}
%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

% Figure mu across all species and climate predictors
\begin{figure}[!ht]
\includegraphics[width=0.8\textwidth]{../../analyses/figures/growthModelsMain/muALLbspp.jpeg}
\caption{Model slope parameters across the different model predictors. GDD represent the accumulated growing degree days between leafout and budset, GSL the number of days between these phases. SOS represent the start of season, which is leafout and EOS, the end of season through budset. All predictors were fit using the same model and same observation, with only the independent variable changing (see Figure \ref{fig:wcSOScomparison} for a comparison between two different data set length for SOS, and Figure \ref{fig:wcEOScomparison}, for end of EOS). The dots represent the mean of the posterior distribution, the thick lines the 50\% uncertainty intervals (UI) and the thinner lines, the 90\% UI.}
\label{fig:wcmuplotbspp}
\end{figure}

\begin{enumerate}

% GDD
\item Growth did not change with greater thermal season for most species 
\Ainc: ($\beta = $ 0.1, $UI_{90}$[-0.1, 0.2]),
\Ball: ($\beta = $ 
0.1, $UI_{90}$[0, 
0.2], and
\Bpop:  ($\beta = $ 
0, $UI_{90}$[-0.1, 
0.2]].
However,
\Bpap grew more with longer thermal season ($\beta = $ 
0.3, $UI_{90}$[0.1, 
0.6])) (Figure \ref{fig:wcmuplotbspp} a) and e)).

% GSL
\item In contrast, most species grew more with longer calendar seasons 
(\Ainc: ($\beta = $ 0.1, $UI_{90}$[0.1, 0.2]),
\Ball: ($\beta = $ 0.1, $UI_{90}$[0, 0.2]) and
\Bpap: ($\beta = $ 0.3, $UI_{90}$[0.1, 0.5]).
Similarly to its response to the thermal season,
\Bpop did not grow more with longer calendar seasons: ($\beta = $ 0, $UI_{90}$[-0.1, 0.1])
(Figure \ref{fig:wcmuplotbspp} b) and f).

% SOS
\item Growth decreased with a later start of season (SOS) for two species
\Ainc ($\beta = $ -0.1, $UI_{90}$[-0.2, -0.1]) and 
\Bpop ($\beta = $ -0.1, $UI_{90}$[-0.2, 0]), but didn't change for the other two 
(\Ball ($\beta = $ 0, $UI_{90}$[-0.1, 0]) and
\Bpap ($\beta = $ -0.1, $UI_{90}$[-0.2, 0])) (Figure \ref{fig:wcmuplotbspp} c) and g)). The estimates were unchanged when using the full or restricted datasets ($n =$239 vs 227). 

% EOS
\item Growth decreased with a later end of season (EOS) only for for 
\Ainc ($\beta = $ -0.1, $UI_{90}$[-0.2, -0.1]). 
Growth for the other species did not change with a later end of season
(\Bpop showed a weaker response but appears to also grow less with a later EOS ($\beta = $ -0.1, $UI_{90}$[-0.2, 0], 
\Ball ($\beta = $ 0.1, $UI_{90}$[0, 0.2]) and
\Bpap ($\beta = $ 0.1, $UI_{90}$[0, 0.3]) (Figure \ref{fig:wcmuplotbspp} d) and g)). The estimates were unchanged when using the full or restricted datasets ($n =$230 vs 227 Figure \ref{fig:}). 

% Site
\item Growth did not change across any sites ( 
S. Hypolyte ($\alpha_{site} = $ 0.1, $UI_{90}$[-0.8, 0.9]),
D. College ($\alpha_{site} = $ -0.1, $UI_{90}$[-0.9, 0.7]), 
W. Mountains ($\alpha_{site} = $ 0.1, $UI_{90}$[-0.7, 1]) and 
H. Forest ($\alpha_{site} = $ = -0.1, $UI_{90}$[-1, 0.7]) 
(Figure \ref{fig:wcmuplotasite}))

% Wildchrokie site mu plot
\begin{figure}[!ht]
\includegraphics[width=1\textwidth]{../../analyses/figures/growthModelsMain/asiteMap.pdf}
\caption{The effect of sites on growth across a latitudinal gradient. a) Model intercept parameters for each site with growing degree days as the predictor. The dots represent the mean of the posterior distribution, the thick lines the 50\% uncertainty intervals (UI) and the thinner lines, the 90\% UI.}
\label{fig:wcmuplotasite}
\end{figure}

Our results show similar trends in response to the thermal (GDD) (Figure \ref{fig:wcgrowthSlopesGDD}) versus the calendar growing season (GSL) (Figure \ref{fig:wcgrowthSlopesGSL}), with a stronger response to GSL. Specifically, \Ainc shows a marked shift in its response strength with longer GSL compared to GDD.

% Effect size on standardized scale

\end{enumerate}
