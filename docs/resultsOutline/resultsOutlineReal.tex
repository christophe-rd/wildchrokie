% Shifted phenology with climate change has extended the vegetative growing season, which is widely expected to increase tree growth. Multiple recent studies, however, have failed to find this relationship and suggested shifts in drought or competition may prevent increased growth. Here, we ask if trees grow more with longer seasons in an environment where drought and competition are limited. We aim to answer this question by leveraging 9 years of vegetative phenology data from two studies located in an urban arboretum and by relating it to tree growth (Figure \ref{fig:chaptersDesign}). We found contrasting evidence that trees grow more during longer seasons. Nine of our species did not change their growth; one grew less at its adult stage but more at its juvenile stage, and two additional species grew more with longer seasons. These responses increased when using the calendar instead of the thermal growing season. Our main findings demonstrate that tree growth responses with climate change may be more complex than previously suggested. The shifts in response to season lengths were not massive, and when they happened it was from juvenile more than mature trees.

\section*{Research questions}
% <><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><>
\begin{enumerate}
\item Does tree growth increase with longer growing seasons?
\item Did an earlier start and later end of season increase growth?
\item Does tree growth increase with longer growing seasons and is it consistent across species and provenances?
\item How did growth change across years, and how did it relate to climate?
\end{enumerate}

% <><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><>
\section*{Results}
% <><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><>
\begin{enumerate}
\item Summary basic information
  \begin{enumerate}
  \item Wildchrokie includes 81 individuals of four deciduous tree species from four sites with leaf phenology data spaning three years (2018 to 2020) for a total of 239 leafout and 230 budset observations, yielding 227 complete growing seasons (GS). 
  
    \item GSL ranged from 
  90 days ({\Ball} in 2018) to 
  173 days ({\Ainc} in 2018)

  \item GS thermal sum ranged from 
  1465.1 GDD ({\Ball} in 2018) to 
  2513.6 GDD, ({\Ainc} in 2018)
  
  \item The earliest leafout day of year was 112 (2019) and the latest, 157 (2019). The earliest budset day of year was 225 (2018) and the latest, 297 (2018) 
  
  \item 2018 had a mean summer temperature of 22.85$^\circ$C and had a mean summer PDSI value of 
  1.92. 2019 was cooler 
  (22.38$^\circ$C) and wetter 
  (PDSI $=$ 1.92), and 2020 was warmer 
  (23.07$^\circ$C) and drier
  (PDSI $=$ -2.35) than 2018.

\end{enumerate}

% Question 1 --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- ---
\item Does tree growth increase with longer growing seasons?
  \begin{enumerate}

  \item Longer growing seasons increased growth for all species, but varied depending on the metric used to measure it.
  
  \item Longer thermal season increased growth for {\Ball} 
  ($\beta = $ 
  0.13, $UI_{90}$
  [0.01, 
  0.24]), {\Bpap} 
  ($\beta = $ 
  0.44, $UI_{90}$
  [0.18, 
  0.7]), and {\Bpop} 
  ($\beta = $ 
  0.22, $UI_{90}$
  [0.07, 
  0.36]), but {\Ainc} trended in the same direction 
  ($\beta = $ 
  0.07, $UI_{90}$
  [-0.04, 
  0.17]) (Figure \ref{fig:wcmuplotbspp}a and e) (Table\ref{tab:wc_slope_table}).
 
  \item Only one out of those three species also grew more with longer calendar season (\Bpap: $\beta = $ 
  0.18, $UI_{90}$
  [0.05, 
  0.32]), but an additional species that didn't grow more with warmer thermal season, did with longer calendar season ({\Ainc}: $\beta = $ 
  0.05, $UI_{90}$
  [0, 
  0.1]).
  \item {\Ball} ($\beta = $ 
  0.03, $UI_{90}$
  [-0.02, 
  0.09]), and {\Bpop} ($\beta = $ 
  0.05, $UI_{90}$
  [-0.02, 
  0.13]) trended in the same direction (Figure \ref{fig:wcmuplotbspp}b and f) (Table \ref{tab:wc_slope_table}).
  
  \item Accounting for the number of days with maximum temperature above 30$^\circ$C, decreased species responses to longer thermal seasons (\textit{results tbd}), and all species grew more with more days with high temperatures (Figure:\ref{fig:muTempAbv30}).
  
  \item Results with basal area increment (BAI) for the GDD model were broadly similar to ring width (see Figure \ref{fig:RWvsBAI})
  \end{enumerate}
  
% Question 2 --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- ---
\item Did an later start and end of season increase growth?
  \begin{enumerate}
  \item A longer calendar season increased growth for two species, but neither grew more with a later start of season. 
  \item {\Ainc} grew more with longer calendar season, but grew less with a later start of season ($\beta = $ 
  -0.07, $UI_{90}$
  [-0.14, 
  -0.01]) (Figure \ref{fig:wcmuplotbspp}c and g) (Table \ref{tab:wc_slope_table}).
  
  \item {\Ball} which did not grow more with longer calendar season, did increase growth with a later start of season ($\beta = $ 
  0.1, $UI_{90}$
  [0, 
  0.2]) (Figure \ref{fig:wcmuplotbspp}c and g) (Table \ref{tab:wc_slope_table}).
  
  \item Three species grew more with a later end of season ({\Ball}: $\beta = $ 
  0.12, $UI_{90}$
  [0.05, 
  0.18], 
  {\Bpop}: $\beta = $ 
  0.1, $UI_{90}$
  [0.02, 
  0.17]), with {\Bpap} 
  ($\beta = $ 
  0.21, $UI_{90}$
  [0.09, 
  0.33])
  being the only one of those three that also grew more with longer calendar season (Figure \ref{fig:wcmuplotbspp}d and h) (Table \ref{tab:wc_slope_table}).
  
  \end{enumerate}

% Question 3 --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- ---
\item Does tree growth increase with longer growing seasons and is it consistent across species and provenances?
  \begin{enumerate}
  
  \item {\Bpap} grew the least ($\alpha = $ 
  -3.82, $UI_{90}$
  [-8.73, 
  1.09]) (Figure \ref{fig:wcmuplotatreeid}), but was the most responsive to longer growing seasons (Figure \ref{fig:wcmuplotbspp}a and b).
  
  \item {\Ainc} grew the most ($\alpha = $  
  1.01, $UI_{90}$
  [-3.14, 
  5.18]) (Figure \ref{fig:wcmuplotatreeid}), but had contrasting responses to longer growing seasons (Figure \ref{fig:wcmuplotbspp}a and b).

  \item Growth did not change across any provenances (Figure \ref{fig:wcmuplotasite}) (Table \ref{tab:wc_asite_table}), but there was slightly more variation across sites ($\sigma = $
  0.18, $UI_{90}$
  [0.02, 
  0.5) than across treeids ($\sigma = $ 
  0.17, $UI_{90}$
  [0.05, 
  0.27]

  \end{enumerate}


% Question 4 --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- ---
\item How did growth change across years, and how did it relate to climate?
  \begin{enumerate}
  
  \item Annual ring widths across years varied from 
  0.39 mm ({\Ball} in 2020) to 
  13.55 mm ({\Bpop} in 2018) (Figure \ref{fig:wcRWboxplot}). 
 
  \item Growth was highest in 2018 
  (0.19, $UI_{90}$
  [-0.76, 
1.1]) (Figure \ref{fig:wcmuplotayear}), but that year had not the warmest nor the longest calendar season, unlike our finding that warmer and longer seasons increase growth for most of our species (Figure \ref{fig:wcxyGDD} and Figure \ref{fig:wcxyGSL}).
  
  \item Growth was lowest in 2020 
  (-0.43, $UI_{90}$
  [-1.38, 
  0.48]) (Figure \ref{fig:wcmuplotayear}) that year had the warmest thermal, but the shortest calendar season, also unlike our findings that growth increases with warmer seasons, and to a lesser extent, with longer calendar season (Figure \ref{fig:wcxyGDD} and Figure \ref{fig:wcxyGSL}).
  \end{enumerate}

\end{enumerate}
