% Shifted phenology with climate change has extended the vegetative growing season, which is widely expected to increase tree growth. Multiple recent studies, however, have failed to find this relationship and suggested shifts in drought or competition may prevent increased growth. Here, we ask if trees grow more with longer seasons in an environment where drought and competition are limited. We aim to answer this question by leveraging 9 years of vegetative phenology data from two studies located in an urban arboretum and by relating it to tree growth (Figure \ref{fig:chaptersDesign}). We found contrasting evidence that trees grow more during longer seasons. Nine of our species did not change their growth; one grew less at its adult stage but more at its juvenile stage, and two additional species grew more with longer seasons. These responses increased when using the calendar instead of the thermal growing season. Our main findings demonstrate that tree growth responses with climate change may be more complex than previously suggested. The shifts in response to season lengths were not massive, and when they happened it was from juvenile more than mature trees.

\item How do species ring widths vary with thermal season vs calendar season?

% <><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><>
\section*{Results}
% <><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><>
\begin{enumerate}
\item Summary results
  \begin{enumerate}
  \item Wildchrokie includes four deciduous tree species from four sites with 227 complete growing seasons (GS)
  
  \item Phenology data span three years from 2018 to 2020 with 239 leafout and 230 budset observations
  \item The earliest leafout day of year was 112 (2019) and the latest, 157 (2019). 
  \item The earliest budset day of year was 225 (2018) and the latest, 297 (2018)
  
  \item GS thermal sum ranged from 
  1465.1 GDD ({\Ball} in 2018) to 
  2513.6 GDD, ({\Ainc} in 2018)
  
  \item GSL ranged from 
  90 days ({\Ball} in 2018) to 
  173 days ({\Ainc} in 2018)
  
  \item Annual ring widths across years varied from 
  0.39 mm ({\Ball} in 2020) to 
  13.55 mm ({\Bpop} in 2018). 
\end{enumerate}

\item Did growth change across years, and if so what cue had the largest effects?
  \begin{enumerate}
  \item Growth was highest on average in 2018, but no cue dominated (it was neither: the wettest; the driest; the shortest; the latest SOS; the earliest EOS nor the highest thermal sum)
  \item Growth was lowest on average in 2020 and that year was: the driest; the shortest; the latest SOS and EOS and had the highest thermal sum
  \end{enumerate}

\item How do species ring widths vary with thermal season vs calendar season?
  \begin{enumerate}
  \item A later start of season decreased growth for two species 
  ({\Ainc}: $\beta = $ , $UI_{90}$
  [, 
  ] and 
  {\Bpop}: $\beta = $ , $UI_{90}$[, ]) (Figure \ref{fig:wcmuplotbspp}c (Table \ref{tab:wc_slope_table}).
  
  \item A later end of season decreased growth only for {\Ainc} 
  ($\beta = $ , $UI_{90}$
  [, ]) (Figure \ref{fig:wcmuplotbspp}d (Table \ref{tab:wc_slope_table}).
  
  \item Longer thermal season increased growth only for {\Bpap} 
  ($\beta = $ , $UI_{90}$
[, 
]) (Figure \ref{fig:wcmuplotbspp}a (Table \ref{tab:wc_slope_table}).

  \item But longer calendar season increased growth for two additional species
  (\Ainc: $\beta = $ , $UI_{90}$[, ],
\Ball: $\beta = $ , $UI_{90}$[, ] and
\Bpap: $\beta = $ , $UI_{90}$[, ]) (Figure \ref{fig:wcmuplotbspp}b (Table \ref{tab:wc_slope_table}).

  \item Calendar season had a bigger effect on growth than the thermal season (Figure \ref{fig:bsppZscored})
  
  \end{enumerate}

\item Does the previous year cummulated GDD influence the following year's growth?
  \begin{enumerate}
  \item Growth decreased for all species with current year GDD
  \item But growth decreased for only two species with previous year GDD
  \end{enumerate}
  
\item How do species spring and autumn phenophases shift with the temperature of different seasons?
  \begin{enumerate}
  \item \textit{results in progress so this is just an example of where it would belong:} warmer winter delayed leafout, but warmer springs advanced leafout.
  \end{enumerate}

\item Does growth change across sites?
  \begin{enumerate}
  \item Growth did not change across any site (Figure \ref{fig:wcmuplotasite}) (Table \ref{tab:wc_asite_table}).
  \end{enumerate}

\end{enumerate}
