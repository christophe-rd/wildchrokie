% Shifted phenology with climate change has extended the vegetative growing season, which is widely expected to increase tree growth. Multiple recent studies, however, have failed to find this relationship and suggested shifts in drought or competition may prevent increased growth. Here, we ask if trees grow more with longer seasons in an environment where drought and competition are limited. We aim to answer this question by leveraging 9 years of vegetative phenology data from two studies located in an urban arboretum and by relating it to tree growth (Figure \ref{fig:chaptersDesign}). We found contrasting evidence that trees grow more during longer seasons. Nine of our species did not change their growth; one grew less at its adult stage but more at its juvenile stage, and two additional species grew more with longer seasons. These responses increased when using the calendar instead of the thermal growing season. Our main findings demonstrate that tree growth responses with climate change may be more complex than previously suggested. The shifts in response to season lengths were not massive, and when they happened it was from juvenile more than mature trees.

\section*{Research questions}
% <><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><>
\begin{enumerate}
\item Does tree growth increase with longer growing seasons?
\item Did an earlier start and later end of season increase growth?
\item Does tree growth increase with longer growing seasons and is it consistent across species and provenances?
\item How did growth change across years, and how did it relate to climate?
\end{enumerate}

\section*{Results}
% <><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><><>
\begin{enumerate}
\item Summary basic information
  \begin{enumerate}
  \item Wildchrokie includes 81 individuals of four deciduous tree species from four sites with 227 complete growing seasons (GS)
  
  \item GSL ranged from 
  90 days ({\Ball} in 2018) to 
  173 days ({\Ainc} in 2018)
  
  \item Phenology data span three years from 2018 to 2020 with 239 leafout and 230 budset observations. The earliest leafout day of year was 112 (2019) and the latest, 157 (2019). The earliest budset day of year was 225 (2018) and the latest, 297 (2018)
  
  \item GS thermal sum ranged from 
  1465.1 GDD ({\Ball} in 2018) to 
  2513.6 GDD, ({\Ainc} in 2018)
  
  % \item No drought for the three years except in 2020, where a moderate summer drought ramped up to a severe drought in September.
  

\end{enumerate}

% Question 1 --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- ---
\item Does tree growth increase with longer growing seasons?
  \begin{enumerate}

  \item Longer growing seasons increased growth for 1-3 species, depending on the metric
  \item Longer thermal season increased growth for {\Bpap} 
  ($\beta = $ 
  0.32, $UI_{90}$
  [0.04, 
  0.6]) (Figure \ref{fig:wcmuplotbspp}a (Table\ref{tab:wc_slope_table}).
 
  \item Calendar season had a bigger effect on growth than the thermal season \ref{fig:bsppZscored}), and two additional species species grew more with longer calendar seasons
  (\Ainc: $\beta = $ 
  0.09, $UI_{90}$
  [0.04, 
  0.14],
  \Ball: $\beta = $ 
  0.07, $UI_{90}$
  [0, 
  0.13] and \Bpap: $\beta = $ 
  0.19, $UI_{90}$
  [0.05, 
  0.33]) (Figure \ref{fig:wcmuplotbspp}b (Table \ref{tab:wc_slope_table}).

  \item Only {\Bpop} showed a clear no effect to longer growing seasons.
  
  \end{enumerate}
  
% Question 2 --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- ---
\item Did an earlier start and later end of season increase growth?
  \begin{enumerate}
  \item A longer calendar season increased growth for three species, but an earlier start of season increased growth only for one of those three
  ({\Ainc}: $\beta = $ 
  -0.18, $UI_{90}$
  [-0.23, 
  -0.13], but also for  {\Bpop}: $\beta = $ 
  -0.13, $UI_{90}$
  [-0.23, 
  -0.02]) (Figure \ref{fig:wcmuplotbspp}c (Table \ref{tab:wc_slope_table}).
  
  \item A later end of season decreased growth only for {\Ainc} 
  ($\beta = $ -0.09, $UI_{90}$
  [-0.15, -0.04]) (Figure \ref{fig:wcmuplotbspp}d (Table \ref{tab:wc_slope_table}).
  
  \end{enumerate}

% Question 3 --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- ---
\item Does tree growth increase with longer growing seasons and is it consistent across species and provenances?
  \begin{enumerate}
  
  \item {\Bpap} grew the least ($\beta = $ 
  -3.31, $UI_{90}$
  [-7.89, 
  1.24]) and {\Bpop} grew the most ($\beta = $  
  1.05, $UI_{90}$
  [-3.04, 
  5.01]) (Figure \ref{fig:wcmuplotatreeid}).

  \item Growth did not change across any site (Figure \ref{fig:wcmuplotasite}) (Table \ref{tab:wc_asite_table}), but there was more variation across sites ($\sigma = $ 0.192) than across treeids ($\sigma = $ 0.099)
  \item But 
  \end{enumerate}

% Question 4 --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- --- ---
\item How did growth change across years, and how did it relate to climate?
  \begin{enumerate}
  \item Growth was highest on average in 2018, but no cue dominated (it was neither: the wettest; the driest; the shortest; the latest SOS; the earliest EOS nor the highest thermal sum)
  \item Growth was lowest on average in 2020 and that year was: the driest; the shortest; the latest SOS and EOS and had the highest thermal sum
  \end{enumerate}

\end{enumerate}
